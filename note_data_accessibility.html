Accessibility Measures for Unpopulated Grid Cells

WP4.6 Characterising rural diversity

In developing the urban–rural typology (WP 4.6), accessibility measures are needed for all 1 km EU Census grid cells, including those without population.

The inclusion of unpopulated areas is essential for us because, even in the absence of residential population, such locations may still host various forms of economic or infrastructural activity. Accurately representing these areas is therefore critical to achieving a comprehensive and more accurate typology.

Ideally, accessibility measures would be derived through a full origin–destination matrix using OSRM. However, given current time and computational constraints, this approach is not feasible for us.

As an alternative, we use a spatial interpolation method, specifically Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW), to estimate accessibility for unpopulated grid cells. Although this approach provides a practical and methodologically sound approximation under the current circumstances, it is not without limitations. In particular, IDW may introduce smoothing effects that reduce local accuracy.

Current Approach: Spatial Smoothing with IDW

To produce a complete dataset suitable for typology development, we used IDW, a spatial interpolation method that estimates missing values based on surrounding observations, giving greater influence to closer points.

We chose IDW because it is conceptually simple, computationally efficient for large datasets, and adaptable to local spatial patterns. It also preserves the original known values through exact interpolation and does not require assumptions about the spatial correlation structure of the data (unlike kriging for example).

The interpolation of travel times measures is performed on log-transformed accessibility values, which are used in typology classification. This transformation stabilizes variance, reduces the effect of extreme outliers, and improves interpolation accuracy, as IDW performs better when data approximate a normal distribution.

The resulting dataset fills over 2.7 million missing values while preserving all known data, creating a seamless 1×1 km resolution surface of accessibility across the entire EU grid.

However, IDW has many limitations. It assumes spatial stationarity (meaning spatial relationships are consistent across the area), uses Euclidean distance (which may not reflect travel networks or topographic barriers), and does not provide uncertainty estimates. The method may also oversmooth local variation in favor of broader regional trends.

Our main concern is that the current approach tends to underestimate accessibility for cells close to major roads. For example, in the Telemark region of Norway, cells near the European highway E134 do not show higher accessibility than those farther away. This is counterintuitive but understandable, since IDW only smooths existing values and does not account for the presence of road networks or other infrastructure.
